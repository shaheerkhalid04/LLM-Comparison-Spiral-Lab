The Rise of Renewable Energy

Renewable energy comes from sources that are naturally replenished, such as
sunlight, wind, water, and geothermal heat. Unlike fossil fuels, which take
millions of years to form and release carbon dioxide when burned, renewable
sources produce little or no greenhouse gas emissions during operation. Over
the past two decades, the cost of solar panels and wind turbines has fallen
sharply, making clean energy competitive with, and in many places cheaper
than, coal and natural gas.

Solar power captures energy from the sun using photovoltaic cells that convert
light directly into electricity. Wind power uses large turbines whose blades
spin as air moves past them, driving a generator. Hydropower relies on moving
water, often stored behind dams, to turn turbines. Each of these technologies
has strengths and limitations. Solar and wind depend on the weather and time
of day, which makes energy storage, usually in large batteries, an important
part of a reliable clean grid.

Governments around the world have set targets to increase the share of
renewables in their energy mix. These policies, combined with private
investment, have driven rapid growth. However, challenges remain: building new
transmission lines, recycling old panels and batteries, and managing the
variable nature of some sources. Despite these hurdles, most analysts agree
that renewable energy will play a central role in reducing global carbon
emissions and slowing climate change in the decades ahead.
