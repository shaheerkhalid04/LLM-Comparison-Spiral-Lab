Renewable energy is derived from naturally replenished sources such as sunlight, wind, water, and geothermal heat, and it generates little or no greenhouse‑gas emissions during operation, unlike fossil fuels that release carbon dioxide when burned. Over the past two decades, the cost of solar panels and wind turbines has dropped sharply, making clean energy competitive with, and often cheaper than, coal and natural gas. Solar power uses photovoltaic cells to convert sunlight directly into electricity; wind power relies on turbines whose blades spin in moving air to drive generators; hydropower harnesses moving water, typically stored behind dams, to turn turbines. Solar and wind are intermittent, depending on weather and time of day, so large‑scale battery storage is important for grid reliability. Governments worldwide have set renewable‑energy targets, and these policies, combined with private investment, have spurred rapid growth. Remaining challenges include building new transmission lines, recycling old panels and batteries, and managing the variability of some sources. Despite these hurdles, most analysts expect renewable energy to play a central role in cutting global carbon emissions and slowing climate change in the coming decades.
